What is hadith?

In the Islamic tradition, hadith refers to the sayings or actions of Prophet Muhammad. After the Qur'an, hadith are considered the most authoritative sources of Islamic law and morality. They were transmitted primarily orally for the first two hundred years or so after the death of Muhammad (10 AH/632 CE) until major efforts at compiling written records took place in the 3rd century AH/9th century CE. While numerous hadith collections have circulated across Islamic history, eventually six of them achieved canonical status in Sunni Islam. Other sects, including the Shi'is, have their own authoritative compilations.

What are the isnād? 

Hadith consists of two parts: the matn (text) and the isnād (chain of transmission). Isnād represents the list of authorities who have transmitted a hadith and are used to evaluate the reliability of each reported hadith. Muslim scholars rate the authenticity of a hadith based on the completion of its chain of transmitters and the reliability of it authorities. 

An example of hadith is as follows,
"Ḥumaydı̄ʿAbd Allāh ibn al‐Zubayr related to us that Sufyān related that Yaḥyā ibn Saʿıd ̄al‐Ansạrī reported: Muhammad ibn Ibrāhım al‐Taymī informed me that he heard 'Ālqama ibn Waqqās al‐Laythī saying: I heard ʿUmar ibn al‐Khaṭṭāb, may Allāh be pleased with him, saying from the minbar: I heard the Apostle of God, peace be upon him, say: “Deeds are according to intentions [niyya], and each person gets what he intends. Whoever emigrated for worldly reward, or for a woman to marry, his emigration is for that for which he emigrated.” (Bukhārī 1 Bādʾ al‐Waḥy, 1)

The highlighted part is the isnād of the hadīth, while the remaining part represents the matn.

Who was Imām Bukhāri?

Abū ʿAbdallāh Muḥammad ibn Ismāʿīl al-Juʿfī al-Bukhārī (194-256 AH/810-870 CE), more commonly known as Imām Bukhāri, Central Asia (now in Uzbekistan), is considered one of the greatest hadīth compilers in the Sunni Islamic history. Born in Bukhara, he studied in Khurāsān and Transoxiana until the age of 16. He then made pilgrimage to Mecca in 201 AH/825 CE and traveled widely for the next 15 years in search of collecting hadith. His collection was compiled in what has now come to known as al-Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī (original title al-Jāmiʿ al-musnad al-ṣaḥīḥ al-mukhtaṣar). It contains almost 7,500 hadith with repetitions and 2,500 without repetitions. The book is divided thematically into 96 chapters.

The Ṣaḥīḥ eventually became part of the six canonical hadith collections in Sunni Islam, which are regarded as such due to the rigorous methods of verification employed by their authors.

Who was 'Ā'isha bint Abī Bakr? 

Born in year 614 CE in Mecca, 'Ā'isha was the third wife of Prophet Muhammad and the daughter of Abū Bakr, one of Muhammad's most important supporters and the first caliph of Islam. She played an important political role after Muhammad's death (10 AH/632 CE) during the reigns of caliphs 'Uthmān and 'Alī. She led an army against 'Alī when he failed to bring 'Uthmān's murderers to justice. However, she was defeated in the Battle of the Camel (36AH/656 CE) against 'Ali and spent the rest of her life quietly in Medina until her death in 58 AH/678 CE.


Due to her closeness to Muhammad, she is considered one of the most important transmitters of hadith in Sunni Islam. In al-Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī, over 500 hadith have been reported on her authority. Out of these, 123 hadith have been randomly selected for this project.

How was the data collected?

Data collection proved to be the most challenging part of this project. While several online hadith databases exist now, almost all present two major issues for researchers. 1) Since modern readers are often not very concerned about complete isnāds, they are often omitted or partially presented. 2) Since almost all these databases serve as popular websites, the accuracy of their content does not always meet the strict scholarly criterions. 

For this project, data was manually scraped from "Prophet (saw) said", a comprehensive online database of hadith with full isnāds. This database was particularly useful since it provided death dates of transmitters as well as the physical locations of the transmitters. While this feature proved to be extremely helpful in making the data more meaningful and generally appeared to be free of errors, its accuracy should still be taken with a grain of salt.

History of the Field

Hadith studies remain one of the most vibrant sub-fields of Islamic Studies in the Western academia. The earliest encounters with hadith literature can be traced as far back as nineteenth century Colonial India. Earliest Western scholarship treated most hadith as later forgeries, and thus not very useful for understanding Islamic history. Ignaz Goldziher (d. 1921) brought a paradigm shift to this view by treating hadīth as important historical documents reflecting the attitudes of the community during its formative period. Until this time, isnāds were not investigated seriously as part of hadīth studies. Next major contribution to hadith studies came from Joseph Schacht (d.1969), who made extensive use of isnāds to locate common links as a way to date hadīth authenticity. According to this theory, hadīth authenticity can be confidently traced back to the common links in different versions of isnāds. This approach was further advanced by Gautier Juynboll (d. 2010) and Herald Motzki (d. 2019), who argued that the common link does not necessarily point to forgery but rather represent collection efforts. More recent contributions in the field have attempted to move beyond the narrow debates over authenticity and instead focus on topics such as authority, canonization, commentaries, non-Sunni hadīth traditions, hadith and other Islamic disciplines, and attitudes towards hadith in the modern period.
